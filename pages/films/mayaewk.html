Me and You and Everyone We Know (2005)

Dir. Miranda July

Miranda July is no stranger to open confrontation with the awkwardness involved in being human, and her debut major film proves no exception. She has constructed here an endearing cast of lonely, often socially impaired but arrestingly sincere characters whom we follow as they struggle to connect with each other in a meaningful way.

July often draws criticism for being unbearably whimsical and insipid, labels which she has noted here seem unshakeable no matter what she does or says. She leans into this rather than allow it to frustrate her, noting that there is freedom in feedback seeming independent of your actions.

“I could say, ‘Yeah, I gangbanged,’ and people would still be like, ‘I don’t know. She seems twee.’ It doesn’t really matter what I do, so I feel pretty free at this point.”

I beg to differ with this particular perception of July, because although her work is certainly very sweet (albeit in an extremely awkward, uncomfortable way) I think there's an obvious self-awareness to it and a sharp sense of comedic timing that cuts through to the core of what she's trying to say.

If my favourite film, Gummo, takes an unrelenting look at the harsh and repulsive realities of the human condition then this film observes and acknowledges that, but answers with hope. It champions vulnerability and points out that our wretched lives are punctuated by moments of perfect clarity which can be meaningful and hopeful if we allow them to matter to us.

July's work feels a continuous embarrassing confession, and how it feels for you will really depend on whether you answer her admission with an empassioned "Oh my God, me too! I thought I was the only one!" or if it fails to land, an unbearable sense of cringe. It's really a matter of personal taste, but I appreciate July being willing to take that kind of artistic risk. In any case, it resonates with me. Worth watching with an open heart.
