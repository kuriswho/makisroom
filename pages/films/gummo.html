Gummo (1997)

Dir. Harmony Korine

What to say about my favourite film of all time? That counterintuitively, I don't like to watch it very often? I don't need to - each of the vignettes this film presents are of sufficient intensity that they are forever burned into my memory. It's an unforgettable film, whether you enjoy it or not.

There's certainly a shock factor for most first-time viewers. I know when I first saw the film, I was struck by the brutality the characters inflicted on themselves and one another. The rot at the core of the film uncomfortably mirrored something unsettling I have often felt within myself.

I'm fascinated and frightened by the knowledge that through boredom, loneliness, desperation or any human being becomes capable of unspeakable cruelty. I'm always a bit incredulous when I think of all the horrible things I could do (and likely already do without realizing) for my own ends or simply through my own willful ignorance.

And yet, director Harmony Korine never condemns his characters or indeed passes any tangible moral judgement - in stark and refreshing contrast with what I am used to seeing in conventional media, he simply allows people to exist as they are. There is no clumsy, pointed lesson to Gummo.
The entire film, off-putting as it may appear, is shot through a lens of love.

In an interview I very much enjoy from November 2000, Korine explains to Independent Pictures Marketing Director Reed Martin what I feel sums up the heart of the film.

"All the characters in my films are beautiful, even the ones that I find disgusting, and I don't see any one person as being any one way. I don't think things are as easy and as simple as they are said or shown to be in most films. For me it wasn't hard or complex showing the complexity of these characters, of a girl with Downs Syndrome, because her beauty is obvious, and transcendent to me. The idea of "exploitation" means nothing to me."

Gummo is unapologetic and holds a steady gaze on the realities we might otherwise instinctively recoil from. It refuses to look away in disgust. In a 1999 interview with Werner Herzog, Korine voices his frustration with some of the filming crew for their complaints about the filming conditions, most notably in residential buildings in Nashville. The film crew demanded protective clothing because of bug infestations and other sanitary concerns, and in rebellion Korine himself and his similarly minded cinematographer Jean-Yves Escoffier filmed several scenes in nothing but Speedos and flip-flops.

It's clear then that the creators of Gummo eschewed any separation between themselves and their subject matter. This is anything but a distant and clinical documentary. Cast in 20 minutes with individuals selected out of Burger Kings and meat factories, it is an extremely raw, horrifying and beautiful series of vignettes of America's poor, neglected and forgotten. It chooses not to make a spectacle of them, but rather champions and lends them a voice. Korine has made a very arresting film and his unpretentious attitude and sharp sense of humor about it only endear me to it more.
